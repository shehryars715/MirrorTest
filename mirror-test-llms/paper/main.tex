\title{What Does the LLM Mirror Test Measure? \\
  Implicit Self-Information Without Explicit Self-Recognition \\
  in Small Open Language Models}

\author{Shehryar \\ NUST, Islamabad \\ \texttt{<email>}}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Large language models have been reported to distinguish their own
generations from those of other models, a behavioral analogue of the
mirror test. We ask when this ability appears and what signal it relies
on. Across five sizes of one open instruction-tuned family (Qwen2.5,
0.5B--14B), three text domains, and four foil authors including humans, we
measure pairwise self-recognition under position counterbalancing and
first-token log-probability scoring (32{,}580 judgments). Verbal
self-recognition against LLM foils is absent to marginal at every size
(pooled accuracy 0.49--0.54) and dominated by extreme position bias; the
ability that does grow with scale is human-versus-machine discrimination
(AUROC 0.58~$\rightarrow$~0.93). Yet a trivial lower-perplexity rule
identifies the judges' own text almost perfectly (99.8\% at $\geq$7B)
while agreeing with the models' actual choices only at chance level
($\kappa \leq 0.04$): the models possess self-information their answers do
not use. A character n-gram classifier (0.90--0.98) outperforms every
judge, and paraphrasing collapses the implicit signal
(0.998~$\rightarrow$~0.712). In this regime, the mirror test measures
position bias and style detection, not self-access. We release all code,
prompts, and judgments.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Since \citet{gallup1970chimpanzees} showed that chimpanzees, but not
monkeys, use a mirror to investigate marks on their own bodies,
mirror-style tests have served as a contested behavioral probe of
self-recognition. A textual analogue has been reported in large language
models: shown two responses to the same task --- one their own, one by
another author --- frontier models identify their own above chance
\citep{panickssery2024llm}. Whether such behavior bears on machine
consciousness is philosophically contested
\citep{butlin2023consciousness,chalmers2023could}; we make no such claim
and use ``self'' throughout only in the prosaic sense of \emph{generated
by the same weights}.

Two questions left open by prior work determine how such results should be
read. First, \textbf{scale}: positive reports concern large proprietary or
7B+ models at single scales; when does the behavior appear within one
training recipe? Second, \textbf{mechanism}: does a model that passes the
mirror test use privileged access to its own generative dispositions, or
something cheaper --- its stylistic fingerprint, or a preference for text
it assigns high probability?

We answer both with a pre-registered, controlled study: five sizes of the
open Qwen2.5-Instruct family (0.5B--14B) judge their own generations
against three LLM foils and human references across news summarization,
open QA, and creative writing, under position counterbalancing,
self-vs-self placebo controls, and log-probability scoring. Mechanism is
probed from three directions: a paraphrase attack, a character-n-gram
stylometric classifier, and a lower-perplexity decision rule evaluated
both as a baseline and as a model of the judges' choices.

The result is a dissociation. Asked directly, the models cannot pick out
their own text from another model's --- accuracy is at or near chance at
every size, and answers are dominated by position preferences so extreme
that individual models choose the same answer letter on up to 99\% of
no-right-answer placebo pairs. What improves with scale is not
self-recognition but \emph{human-versus-machine} discrimination (AUROC
0.58~$\rightarrow$~0.93 against human foils). Meanwhile, the information
needed to pass the test is demonstrably inside the models: choosing
whichever candidate has lower perplexity under the judge identifies its
own text at 99.8\% for the two largest judges --- yet this rule agrees
with the models' actual choices at chance level ($\kappa \leq 0.04$).
Small open models \emph{have} near-perfect implicit self-information and
fail to deploy it in their explicit self-reports.

\noindent Our contributions:
\begin{itemize}\itemsep0pt
  \item the first self-recognition \textbf{scale curve} within a single
        open family (0.5B--14B), with confidence intervals, a
        threshold-free (AUROC) companion metric, and full position-bias
        accounting;
  \item an \textbf{implicit--explicit dissociation}: near-perfect
        likelihood-based self-identification (0.998) coexisting with
        chance-level verbal self-recognition ($\kappa \leq 0.04$ between
        the two);
  \item \textbf{mechanism controls} --- paraphrase, stylometry, likelihood
        preference --- showing the implicit signal is largely
        surface-lexical (0.998~$\rightarrow$~0.712 under paraphrase) and
        that a bag-of-character-n-grams classifier outperforms every judge
        on identical items;
  \item a released pipeline: frozen prompts, seeds, pinned model
        revisions, and all raw judgments, reproducing every figure with
        one command.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\paragraph{Self-recognition in LLMs.}
\citet{panickssery2024llm} established out-of-the-box self-recognition in
GPT-4 and Llama-2 on summarization and linked recognition to
self-preference under fine-tuning; our design is a small-model, controlled
replication and extension of their pairwise and individual protocols.
\citet{davidson2024self} framed recognition between interacting agents as
a safety question; \citet{laine2024me} included continuation-based
self-recognition in a broader situational-awareness benchmark and found
sensitivity to prompt phrasing, motivating our multi-phrasing check.
\citet{zhou2025implicit} report that instruction-tuned models succeed
where base checkpoints fail; our base-vs-instruct ablation tests this
claim on a second family and finds instead that the usable signal is
largely present before instruction tuning.

\paragraph{LLM-judge biases.}
LLM judges exhibit position and verbosity biases \citep{zheng2023judging}.
We counterbalance every pair across both orders, length-match candidates
to within 25\%, and measure residual position bias directly with
self-vs-self placebo pairs --- which proves decisive: several of our
judges choose one answer letter on 90--99\% of placebo pairs, far beyond
the position effects reported for larger chat models.

\paragraph{Detection and metric artifacts.}
Our perplexity rule is a relative of likelihood-based machine-text
detection \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt}, and our human-foil results
connect self-recognition to that literature. \citet{schaeffer2023emergent}
showed apparent emergent jumps can be artifacts of thresholded metrics; we
therefore report AUROC computed from log-probability margins alongside
accuracy throughout.

\section{Method}
\paragraph{Models.}
Judges: Qwen2.5-Instruct at 0.5B, 1.5B, 3B, 7B, and 14B parameters, loaded
in 4-bit NF4 so token log-probabilities remain accessible; exact revisions
are pinned and listed in App.~\ref{app:repro}. Foils:
Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Gemma-2-9B-it, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 ---
three families with distinct alignment styles --- plus human reference
texts. Phi-3.5-mini (never judge or foil) paraphrases; all-MiniLM-L6-v2
checks paraphrase fidelity. The base (non-instruct) 7B and 14B checkpoints
serve an ablation, judging their instruct siblings' pairs through a
completion-style prompt.

\paragraph{Data.}
200 prompts per domain, sampled with a fixed seed, filtered for length and
language, then frozen (SHA-256 checksums committed before any generation):
news summarization (CNN/DailyMail \citealp{hermann2015teaching}; gold
highlights provide the human class), open QA
\citep[Dolly-15k;][]{conover2023dolly}, and story openings
\citep[WritingPrompts;][]{fan2018hierarchical}, with human stories
truncated at a sentence boundary near 120 words. Human references span
40--160 words.

\paragraph{Generation and pairing.}
Every judge and foil answers all 600 prompts (temperature 0.8, top-$p$
0.95, $\leq$160 new tokens, seed $= 1000 + \text{prompt index}$; judges
produce a second, differently-seeded sample for 150 prompts per domain to
build placebo pairs). All texts --- including human references --- pass
one identical cleaning step removing chat artifacts, boilerplate openers,
and truncated final sentences; 91--100\% of texts survive. Pairs are
same-prompt, length-matched ($\leq$25\% word-count difference) and
deduplicated (ROUGE-L $\leq 0.7$; no pair was removed by this filter),
yielding 41--195 pairs per (judge, foil, domain) cell --- human cells are
smallest because humans write shorter than models --- and 204--542 pairs
per judge$\times$foil after pooling domains (App.~\ref{app:cells}).

\paragraph{Recognition protocols.}
In the pairwise protocol (PPP, primary) the judge sees the task and two
responses labeled A and B, one its own, and is asked which it wrote. Every
pair runs in both orders; the item score is the mean of the two runs, so
pure position preference produces ties, not wins. Decisions are read from
first-token log-probabilities --- $\log p(\text{``A''})$ vs
$\log p(\text{``B''})$, summed over tokenizer spellings --- which
eliminates unparseable answers and yields a continuous margin; greedy
free-text answers are also logged on a subsample for refusal accounting.
The instruction template (App.~\ref{app:prompts}) is used verbatim; the
headline cell is re-run under three phrasings. In the individual protocol
(IPP, secondary) the judge sees one text and answers Yes/No (300 balanced
items per judge), analysed with signal detection.

\paragraph{Controls and baselines.}
(i) \emph{Placebo}: self-vs-self pairs measure pure position bias.
(ii) \emph{Stylometric classifier}: TF-IDF over character 3--5-grams with
logistic regression under 5-fold grouped cross-validation (a pair never
spans folds); its pair-level decision picks the candidate with the higher
out-of-fold probability of judge authorship; a surface-features variant
and cross-domain transfer test accompany it. (iii) \emph{Perplexity rule}:
choose the candidate with lower mean per-token negative log-likelihood
under the judge, conditioned on the original task; we report its accuracy
and its agreement (Cohen's $\kappa$) with the judge's choices.
(iv) \emph{Paraphrase attack}: both texts of every pair in the
14B$\times$Llama cells are rewritten by the paraphraser (kept only if
cosine $\geq 0.80$ to the original and within 30\% length; 444 of 542
pairs survive) and re-judged on identical items.

\paragraph{Statistics.}
95\% Wilson intervals on every accuracy. Significance against chance uses
exact binomial tests on decisively answered items (consistent across both
orders --- ties carry no authorship evidence), Holm-corrected across the
20 judge$\times$foil cells. Original-vs-paraphrased and
judge-vs-classifier comparisons use exact McNemar tests on identical
items. AUROC is computed over runs from the score
$\log p(\text{A}) - \log p(\text{B})$ with label ``self shown in position
A'' (position-invariant by construction), with 1{,}000-resample bootstrap
CIs over pairs; $\kappa$ likewise. With $n{=}150$ per cell the exact
binomial test has 79\% power against true accuracy 0.615; pooled cells
($n{=}450$) reach the same power at 0.567 (App.~\ref{app:power}).

\paragraph{Position bias dominates the explicit test.}
On placebo pairs --- both texts written by the judge --- models do not
hover near 50\%: the 0.5B and 7B choose letter ``A'' on 98.9\% of runs,
the 1.5B chooses ``B'' on 90.3\%, the 3B ``B'' on 65.1\%, and the 14B
``A'' on 72.0\% (all CIs exclude 0.5; App.~\ref{app:bias}). The bias is
order-invariant, i.e.\ attached to the letter, not the text.
Counterbalancing absorbs it --- position-consistent answering yields ties
--- but leaves only 1--44\% of items decisively answered (0.5B: 1.0\%;
14B: 44.1\%). Against LLM foils, order-averaged accuracy is then at or
near chance at every size: pooled over the three LLM foils, 0.502 (0.5B),
0.508 (1.5B), 0.492 (3B), 0.505 (7B), 0.540 (14B). Six of twenty
judge$\times$foil cells survive Holm correction
(Table~\ref{tab:main}), all at 3B or larger: the three human-foil cells at
3B--14B, plus 7B--Mistral, 14B--Llama, and 14B--Mistral.

\paragraph{What scales is human-versus-machine discrimination.}
Against human foils, discrimination rises monotonically with size ---
AUROC 0.582, 0.687, 0.668, 0.771, 0.932 (Spearman $\rho = 0.9$; accuracy
$\rho = 1.0$), reaching 0.822 [0.772, 0.863] accuracy at 14B
(Fig.~\ref{fig:scale}). No comparable trend exists against LLM foils
(AUROC $\rho$ from $-0.4$ to $0.5$ and accuracy $\rho$ from $-0.7$ to
$0.7$, with signs disagreeing across foils), and accuracy and AUROC move
together, so the growth is not a thresholding artifact
\citep{schaeffer2023emergent}. The models are increasingly able to tell
machine text from human text --- a capability continuous with
machine-generated-text detection \citep{mitchell2023detectgpt} --- rather
than \emph{their own} text from another model's. One foil produces
systematic \emph{inversion}: against Gemma-2-9B-it, margin-AUROC falls
significantly below chance for the 7B (0.361 [0.326, 0.394]) and the 14B
(0.457 [0.425, 0.485]), with the 3B's accuracy pointing the same way
(0.459 [0.414, 0.504]; Holm-corrected $p = .074$) --- the judges' graded
preference treats Gemma's text as more ``self'' than their own.

\paragraph{A trivial classifier beats every judge.}
The character-n-gram classifier's pair-level accuracy is 0.90--0.98 in
every cell --- higher than every judge in every cell, including the 14B's
best (McNemar $p < .001$ in all 20 comparisons; Table~\ref{tab:main}).
Even the surface-features variant (sentence length, punctuation rates,
type-token ratio) reaches 0.65--0.98 single-text accuracy, and classifiers
trained on one domain transfer above chance to the others (0.54--0.65
single-text for the headline judge--foil pair), so the authorship
signature is model-general surface style. By the
pre-registered interpretation rule, the information required for
``self-recognition'' is present in surface statistics, and privileged
self-access is not required to explain any positive result here.

\paragraph{The models have the information and do not use it.}
The lower-perplexity rule --- pick the candidate more probable under the
judge's own weights, given the task --- identifies SELF with pooled
accuracy 0.795 (0.5B), 0.661 (1.5B), 0.970 (3B), 0.998 (7B), and 0.998
(14B). Near-ceiling performance is itself unsurprising --- the self text
was \emph{sampled from} the judge, so high self-likelihood borders on
tautology (cf.\ likelihood-based detection;
\citealp{mitchell2023detectgpt}) --- but that is exactly what makes the
rule diagnostic: it upper-bounds what any decision process consulting the
model's own likelihoods could achieve, and for every judge $\geq$3B the
rule exceeds the judge's own accuracy by 40+ points on identical items. A
judge actually consulting its likelihoods would sit near 0.97--1.00; none
comes close. Agreement statistics point the same way but must be read with
care: $|\kappa| \leq 0.037$ in all 20 cells (bootstrap CIs hugging zero),
yet $\kappa$ is partly mechanical here, since agreement measures saturate
toward zero when either party is near-constant --- and both saturations
occur (the rule almost always answers ``self'' at $\geq$3B; the 0.5B and
7B almost always answer one letter). The pre-registered $\kappa > 0.4$
test of H4 was therefore incapable of succeeding in this regime; the
accuracy gap, not $\kappa$, carries the dissociation. It is sharpest for
Gemma: the rule says ``self'' with accuracy 1.000 while the judges' graded
preference runs \emph{below} chance, so the explicit channel is not
tracking likelihood at all. The implicit signal also predates alignment:
base (non-instruct) 7B and 14B checkpoints score 0.864 and 0.860 under the
same rule, and the base 14B already separates its sibling's text from
human text at 0.842 AUROC --- in tension, for this family, with the report
that base models fail where instruct models succeed
\citep{zhou2025implicit}, though our base judging necessarily uses a
completion-style prompt rather than a chat instruction.

\paragraph{Paraphrase locates the signal in surface form.}
Rewriting both candidates (content preserved) collapses the implicit rule
from 0.998 [0.990, 1.000] to 0.712 [0.668, 0.752] on the surviving 444
pairs --- most of the self-likelihood signal is wording, though a reliable
residue survives. The verbal channel shows no significant change (pooled
0.587 $\rightarrow$ 0.544; McNemar $b{=}52$, $c{=}45$, $p = .54$;
per-domain $p = .42$--$.86$; Fig.~\ref{fig:para}) --- there was no
reliable verbal signal to destroy in this cell.

\paragraph{Secondary analyses.}
The Yes/No protocol collapses into pure response bias at every size: the
0.5B answers ``Yes, I wrote this'' to 142 of its own 150 texts (95\%) and
133 of 150 others' (89\%), while the 3B, 7B, and 14B answer ``Yes'' to at
most 1\% of anything
(the 7B says yes to 0 of its own 150 texts and 2 foils'); sensitivity is
uninterpretable at such degenerate criteria, and margin-based AUROC stays
$\leq 0.62$. Accuracy alone would have called the 0.5B a 94\%-hit-rate
self-recognizer --- the signal-detection framing exists precisely for
this. Refusals: 0.0\% of 1{,}732 logged free-text answers, with
free-text--log-probability agreement of 87--99\%. Instruction
re-phrasings move the headline cell's accuracy by 0.065 (0.510--0.575),
consistent with \citet{laine2024me}.

\section{Discussion}
Three channels, three answers. The models' \emph{likelihoods} contain
near-perfect self-information (0.998). Their \emph{forced-choice answers}
are ruled by letter-level position bias, are matched or beaten by a
bag-of-character-n-grams, and do not correlate with their own likelihoods
($\kappa \approx 0$). And the component that genuinely strengthens with
scale is \emph{human-versus-machine} style discrimination, present already
in base checkpoints. On this evidence, passing or failing the behavioral
mirror test in this regime licenses no claim about self-access: the test
measures position bias, generic machine-text detection, and surface style
--- each of which any competent next-token predictor acquires without
anything self-like. Our pre-registered prediction (a deflationary outcome)
was right that surface statistics suffice and that the implicit signal is
largely lexical, but its specific mechanism test failed twice over: verbal
choices are not likelihood expression (the 40-point accuracy gap), and the
$\kappa > 0.4$ criterion we pre-registered could not have detected such
expression anyway once the rule saturated (\S4) --- a design lesson for
future mirror-test studies.

The dissociation matters practically. Self-preference bias in LLM
evaluators has been attributed to self-recognition
\citep{panickssery2024llm}; our results suggest the operative substrate
need not be any explicit recognition --- likelihood-level familiarity
exists in abundance even where verbal recognition is absent, so evaluator
contamination cannot be ruled out by showing a model ``cannot recognize
itself.'' Behavioral mirror tests without mechanism controls cannot
license claims about machine self-awareness; we release the full pipeline
so stronger versions of the test can be built.

\section*{Limitations}
Our findings are behavioral; nothing here measures subjective experience,
and ``self'' denotes only text generated by the same weights. The judge
has no episodic memory of writing; the test can only measure
identification of self-\emph{typical} text. Scale conclusions are within
one training recipe (Qwen2.5) and one decoding setting (temperature 0.8
generation, greedy judging). The paraphrase attack covers one
representative cell (14B$\times$Llama, three domains); the base-model
ablation defines SELF as the instruct sibling's text and uses a
completion-style prompt, two deliberate interpretive choices that limit
direct comparison with chat-prompted judges. Cohen's $\kappa$ between
judges and the perplexity rule is reported for completeness but is
uninformative under the observed marginal saturation (\S4). Human-foil
cells are smaller (41--147 pairs per domain) because length-matching removes verbose-model-vs-concise-human
pairs. Prompt datasets likely occur in all models' pretraining data
(shared across authors, but uncontrolled). Extreme response biases make
IPP sensitivity indices uninformative at several sizes. All materials are
English; per-cell power detects effects $\geq{\sim}6$ points above chance,
and smaller effects may be missed.

\section{Reproducibility}\label{app:repro}
Model Hugging Face ids with pinned commit revisions, decoding parameters,
and the seed scheme (dataset sampling 42; generation $1000+$prompt index;
placebo $2000+$index; paraphrase $3000+$index) are in
\texttt{configs/models.yaml}. All experiments ran in 4-bit NF4 on free-tier
GPUs (Colab T4, Kaggle 2$\times$T4; $\approx$53 GPU-hours total).
\texttt{python src/06\_stats.py} reproduces every number, table, and
figure from the released raw judgments (32{,}580 pairwise runs, 1{,}500
Yes/No items, 12{,}114 perplexity scores).

\section{Power Analysis}\label{app:power}
Exact binomial test, $\alpha = .05$, two-sided: $n{=}150$ gives power
0.788 at true accuracy 0.615; $n{=}450$ (pooled domains) gives 0.795 at
0.567; $n{=}780$ gives 0.796 at 0.550.
