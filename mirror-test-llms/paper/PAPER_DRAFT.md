## Abstract

Large language models have been reported to distinguish their own
generations from those of other models, a behavioral analogue of the mirror
test. We ask when this ability appears and what signal it relies on. Across
five sizes of one open instruction-tuned family (Qwen2.5, 0.5B–14B), three
text domains, and four foil authors including humans, we measure pairwise
self-recognition under position counterbalancing and first-token
log-probability scoring (32,580 judgments). Verbal self-recognition against
LLM foils is absent to marginal at every size (pooled accuracy 0.49–0.54)
and dominated by extreme position bias; the ability that does grow with
scale is human-versus-machine discrimination (AUROC 0.58 → 0.93). Yet a
trivial lower-perplexity rule identifies the judges' own text almost
perfectly (99.8% at ≥7B) while agreeing with the models' actual choices only
at chance level (κ ≤ 0.04): the models possess self-information their
answers do not use. A character n-gram classifier (0.90–0.98) outperforms
every judge, and paraphrasing collapses the implicit signal (0.998 → 0.712).
In this regime, the mirror test measures position bias and style detection,
not self-access. We release all code, prompts, and judgments.

## 1 Introduction

Since Gallup (1970) showed that chimpanzees, but not monkeys, use a mirror
to investigate marks on their own bodies, mirror-style tests have served as
a contested behavioral probe of self-recognition. A textual analogue has
been reported in large language models: shown two responses to the same
task — one their own, one by another author — frontier models identify
their own above chance (Panickssery et al., 2024). Whether such behavior
bears on machine consciousness is philosophically contested (Butlin et al.,
2023; Chalmers, 2023); we make no such claim and use "self" throughout only
in the prosaic sense of *generated by the same weights*.

Two questions left open by prior work determine how such results should be
read. First, **scale**: positive reports concern large proprietary or 7B+
models at single scales; when does the behavior appear within one training
recipe? Second, **mechanism**: does a model that passes the mirror test use
privileged access to its own generative dispositions, or something cheaper —
its stylistic fingerprint, or a preference for text it assigns high
probability?

We answer both with a pre-registered, controlled study: five sizes of the
open Qwen2.5-Instruct family (0.5B–14B) judge their own generations against
three LLM foils and human references across news summarization, open QA,
and creative writing, under position counterbalancing, self-vs-self placebo
controls, and log-probability scoring. Mechanism is probed from three
directions: a paraphrase attack, a character-n-gram stylometric classifier,
and a lower-perplexity decision rule evaluated both as a baseline and as a
model of the judges' choices.

The result is a dissociation. Asked directly, the models cannot pick out
their own text from another model's — accuracy is at or near chance at
every size, and the answers are dominated by position preferences so
extreme that individual models choose the same answer letter on up to 99%
of no-right-answer placebo pairs. What improves with scale is not
self-recognition but *human-versus-machine* discrimination (AUROC 0.58 →
0.93 against human foils). Meanwhile, the information needed to pass the
test is demonstrably inside the models: choosing whichever candidate has
lower perplexity under the judge identifies its own text at 99.8% for the
two largest judges — yet this rule agrees with the models' actual choices
at chance level (κ ≤ 0.04). Small open models *have* near-perfect implicit
self-information and fail to deploy it in their explicit self-reports.

Our contributions:

* the first self-recognition **scale curve** within a single open family
  (0.5B–14B), reported with confidence intervals, a threshold-free (AUROC)
  companion metric, and full position-bias accounting;
* an **implicit–explicit dissociation**: near-perfect likelihood-based
  self-identification (0.998) coexisting with chance-level verbal
  self-recognition (κ ≤ 0.04 between the two);
* **mechanism controls** — paraphrase, stylometry, likelihood preference —
  showing the implicit signal is largely surface-lexical (0.998 → 0.712
  under paraphrase) and that a bag-of-character-n-grams classifier
  outperforms every judge on identical items;
* a released pipeline: frozen prompts, seeds, pinned model revisions, and
  all raw judgments, reproducing every figure with one command.

## 2 Related Work

**Self-recognition in LLMs.** Panickssery et al. (2024) established
out-of-the-box self-recognition in GPT-4 and Llama-2 on summarization and
linked recognition to self-preference under fine-tuning; our design is a
small-model, controlled replication and extension of their pairwise and
individual protocols. Davidson et al. (2024) framed recognition between
interacting agents as a safety question; Laine et al. (2024) included
continuation-based self-recognition in a broader situational-awareness
benchmark and found sensitivity to prompt phrasing, motivating our
multi-phrasing check. Zhou et al. (2025) report that instruction-tuned
models succeed where base checkpoints fail; our base-vs-instruct ablation
tests this claim on a second family and finds instead that the usable
signal is largely present before instruction tuning.

**LLM-judge biases.** LLM judges exhibit position and verbosity biases
(Zheng et al., 2023). We counterbalance every pair across both orders,
length-match candidates to within 25%, and measure residual position bias
directly with self-vs-self placebo pairs — which proves decisive: several
of our judges choose one answer letter on 90–99% of placebo pairs, far
beyond the position effects reported for larger chat models.

**Detection and metric artifacts.** Our perplexity rule is a relative of
likelihood-based machine-text detection (Mitchell et al., 2023), and our
human-foil results connect self-recognition to that literature. Schaeffer
et al. (2023) showed apparent emergent jumps can be artifacts of
thresholded metrics; we therefore report AUROC computed from log-probability
margins alongside accuracy throughout.

## 3 Method

**Models.** Judges: Qwen2.5-Instruct at 0.5B, 1.5B, 3B, 7B, and 14B
parameters, loaded in 4-bit NF4 so token log-probabilities remain
accessible; exact revisions are pinned and listed in Appendix E. Foils:
Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Gemma-2-9B-it, and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 — three
families with distinct alignment styles — plus human reference texts.
Phi-3.5-mini (never judge or foil) paraphrases; all-MiniLM-L6-v2 checks
paraphrase fidelity. The base (non-instruct) 7B and 14B checkpoints serve
an ablation, judging their instruct siblings' pairs through a
completion-style prompt.

**Data.** 200 prompts per domain, sampled with a fixed seed, filtered for
length and language, then frozen (SHA-256 checksums committed before any
generation): news summarization (CNN/DailyMail; gold highlights provide the
human class), open QA (Dolly-15k), and story openings (WritingPrompts;
human stories truncated at a sentence boundary near 120 words). Human
references span 40–160 words.

**Generation and pairing.** Every judge and foil answers all 600 prompts
(temperature 0.8, top-p 0.95, ≤160 new tokens, seed = 1000 + prompt index;
judges produce a second, differently-seeded sample for 150 prompts per
domain to build placebo pairs). All texts — including human references —
pass one identical cleaning step removing chat artifacts, boilerplate
openers, and truncated final sentences; 91–100% of texts survive. Pairs are
same-prompt, length-matched (≤25% word-count difference) and deduplicated
(ROUGE-L ≤ 0.7; no pair was removed by this filter), yielding 41–195 pairs
per (judge, foil, domain) cell — human cells are smallest because humans
write shorter than models — and 204–542 pairs per judge×foil after pooling
domains (exact counts in Appendix B).

**Recognition protocols.** In the pairwise protocol (PPP, primary) the
judge sees the task and two responses labeled A and B, one its own, and is
asked which it wrote. Every pair runs in both orders; the item score is the
mean of the two runs, so pure position preference produces ties, not wins.
Decisions are read from first-token log-probabilities — log p("A") vs
log p("B"), summed over tokenizer spellings — which eliminates unparseable
answers and yields a continuous margin; greedy free-text answers are also
logged on a subsample for refusal accounting. The instruction template
(Appendix A) is verbatim from the protocol; the headline cell is re-run
under three phrasings. In the individual protocol (IPP, secondary) the
judge sees one text and answers Yes/No (300 balanced items per judge),
analysed with signal detection.

**Controls and baselines.** (i) *Placebo*: self-vs-self pairs measure pure
position bias. (ii) *Stylometric classifier*: TF-IDF over character
3–5-grams with logistic regression under 5-fold grouped cross-validation
(a pair never spans folds); its pair-level decision picks the candidate
with the higher out-of-fold probability of judge authorship; a
surface-features variant and cross-domain transfer test accompany it.
(iii) *Perplexity rule*: choose the candidate with lower mean per-token
negative log-likelihood under the judge, conditioned on the original task;
we report its accuracy and its agreement (Cohen's κ) with the judge's
choices. (iv) *Paraphrase attack*: both texts of every pair in the
14B×Llama cells are rewritten by the paraphraser (kept only if cosine ≥
0.80 to the original and within 30% length; 444 of 542 pairs survive) and
re-judged on identical items.

**Statistics.** 95% Wilson intervals on every accuracy. Significance
against chance uses exact binomial tests on decisively answered items
(consistent across both orders — ties carry no authorship evidence),
Holm-corrected across the 20 judge×foil cells. Original-vs-paraphrased and
judge-vs-classifier comparisons use exact McNemar tests on identical items.
AUROC is computed over runs from the score log p(A) − log p(B) with label
"self shown in position A" (position-invariant by construction), with
1,000-resample bootstrap CIs over pairs; κ likewise. With n = 150 per cell
the exact binomial test has 79% power against true accuracy 0.615; pooled
cells (n = 450) reach the same power at 0.567 (Appendix F).

## 4 Results

**Position bias dominates the explicit test.** On placebo pairs — both
texts written by the judge — models do not hover near 50%: the 0.5B and 7B
choose letter "A" on 98.9% of runs, the 1.5B chooses "B" on 90.3%, the 3B
"B" on 65.1%, and the 14B "A" on 72.0% (all CIs exclude 0.5; Appendix C).
The bias is order-invariant, i.e. attached to the letter, not the text.
Counterbalancing absorbs it — position-consistent answering yields ties —
but it leaves only 1–44% of items decisively answered (0.5B: 1.0%; 14B:
44.1%). Against LLM foils, order-averaged accuracy is then at or near
chance at every size: pooled over the three LLM foils, 0.502 (0.5B), 0.508
(1.5B), 0.492 (3B), 0.505 (7B), 0.540 (14B). Six of twenty judge×foil
cells survive Holm correction (Table 1), all at 3B or larger: the three
human-foil cells at 3B–14B, plus 7B–Mistral, 14B–Llama, and 14B–Mistral.

**What scales is human-versus-machine discrimination.** Against human
foils, discrimination rises monotonically with size — AUROC 0.582, 0.687,
0.668, 0.771, 0.932 (Spearman ρ = 0.9; accuracy ρ = 1.0), reaching 0.822
[0.772, 0.863] accuracy at 14B (Figure 1). No comparable trend exists
against LLM foils (AUROC ρ from −0.4 to 0.5 and accuracy ρ from −0.7 to
0.7, with signs disagreeing across foils), and both accuracy and AUROC
move together, so the growth is not a thresholding artifact (Schaeffer et
al., 2023). The models are increasingly able to tell machine text from
human text — a capability continuous with machine-generated-text detection
(Mitchell et al., 2023) — rather than *their own* text from another
model's. One foil produces systematic *inversion*: against Gemma-2-9B-it,
margin-AUROC falls significantly below chance for the 7B (0.361 [0.326,
0.394]) and the 14B (0.457 [0.425, 0.485]), with the 3B's accuracy
pointing the same way (0.459 [0.414, 0.504]; Holm-corrected p = .074) —
the judges' graded preference treats Gemma's text as more "self" than
their own.

**A trivial classifier beats every judge.** The character-n-gram
classifier's pair-level accuracy is 0.90–0.98 in every cell — higher than
every judge in every cell, including the 14B's best (McNemar p < .001 in
all 20 comparisons; Table 1). Even the surface-features variant (sentence
length, punctuation rates, type-token ratio) reaches 0.65–0.98
single-text accuracy, and classifiers trained on one domain transfer above
chance to the others (0.54–0.65 single-text for the headline judge–foil
pair), so the authorship signature is model-general surface style. By the pre-registered interpretation rule,
the information required for "self-recognition" is present in surface
statistics, and privileged self-access is not required to explain any
positive result here.

**The models have the information and do not use it.** The lower-perplexity
rule — pick the candidate more probable under the judge's own weights,
given the task — identifies SELF with pooled accuracy 0.795 (0.5B), 0.661
(1.5B), 0.970 (3B), 0.998 (7B), and 0.998 (14B). Near-ceiling performance
is itself unsurprising — the self text was *sampled from* the judge, so
high self-likelihood borders on tautology (cf. likelihood-based detection;
Mitchell et al., 2023) — but that is exactly what makes the rule
diagnostic: it upper-bounds what any decision process consulting the
model's own likelihoods could achieve, and for every judge ≥3B the rule
exceeds the judge's own accuracy by 40+ points on identical items. A judge
actually consulting its likelihoods would sit near 0.97–1.00; none comes
close. Agreement statistics point the same way but must be read with care:
|κ| ≤ 0.037 in all 20 cells (bootstrap CIs hugging zero), yet κ is partly
mechanical here, since agreement measures saturate toward zero when either
party is near-constant — and both saturations occur (the rule almost
always answers "self" at ≥3B; the 0.5B and 7B almost always answer one
letter). The pre-registered κ > 0.4 test of H4 was therefore incapable of
succeeding in this regime; the accuracy gap, not κ, carries the
dissociation. It is sharpest for Gemma: the rule says "self" with accuracy
1.000 while the judges' graded preference runs *below* chance, so the
explicit channel is not tracking likelihood at all. The implicit signal
also predates alignment: base (non-instruct) 7B and 14B checkpoints score
0.864 and 0.860 under the same rule, and the base 14B already separates
its sibling's text from human text at 0.842 AUROC — in tension, for this
family, with the report that base models fail where instruct models
succeed (Zhou et al., 2025), though our base judging necessarily uses a
completion-style prompt rather than a chat instruction.

**Paraphrase locates the signal in surface form.** Rewriting both
candidates (content preserved) collapses the implicit rule from 0.998
[0.990, 1.000] to 0.712 [0.668, 0.752] on the surviving 444 pairs — most
of the self-likelihood signal is wording, though a reliable residue
survives. The verbal channel shows no significant change (pooled 0.587 →
0.544; McNemar b = 52, c = 45, p = .54; per-domain p = .42–.86; Figure 2)
— there was no reliable verbal signal to destroy in this cell.

**Secondary analyses.** The Yes/No protocol collapses into pure response
bias at every size: the 0.5B answers "Yes, I wrote this" to 142 of its own
150 texts (95%) and 133 of 150 others' (89%), while the 3B, 7B, and 14B answer "Yes" to at
most 1% of anything (the 7B says yes to 0 of its own 150 texts and 2
foils'); sensitivity is uninterpretable at such degenerate criteria, and
margin-based AUROC stays ≤0.62. Accuracy alone would have called the 0.5B
a 94%-hit-rate self-recognizer — the protocol's signal-detection framing
exists precisely for this. Refusals: 0.0% of 1,732 logged free-text
answers, with free-text/log-probability agreement of 87–99%. Instruction
re-phrasings move the headline cell's accuracy by 0.065 (0.510–0.575),
consistent with Laine et al. (2024).

## 5 Discussion

Three channels, three answers. The models' *likelihoods* contain
near-perfect self-information (0.998). Their *forced-choice answers* are
ruled by letter-level position bias, are matched or beaten by a
bag-of-character-n-grams, and do not correlate with their own likelihoods
(κ ≈ 0). And the component that genuinely strengthens with scale is
*human-versus-machine* style discrimination, present already in base
checkpoints. On this evidence, passing or failing the behavioral mirror
test in this regime licenses no claim about self-access: the test measures
position bias, generic machine-text detection, and surface style — each of
which any competent next-token predictor acquires without anything
self-like. Our pre-registered prediction (a deflationary outcome) was right
that surface statistics suffice and that the implicit signal is largely
lexical, but its specific mechanism test failed twice over: verbal choices
are not likelihood expression (the 40-point accuracy gap), and the κ > 0.4
criterion we pre-registered could not have detected such expression anyway
once the rule saturated (§4) — a design lesson for future mirror-test
studies.

The dissociation matters practically. Self-preference bias in LLM
evaluators has been attributed to self-recognition (Panickssery et al.,
2024); our results suggest the operative substrate need not be any
explicit recognition — likelihood-level familiarity exists in abundance
even where verbal recognition is absent, so evaluator contamination cannot
be ruled out by showing a model "cannot recognize itself." Behavioral
mirror tests without mechanism controls cannot license claims about
machine self-awareness; we release the full pipeline so stronger versions
of the test can be built.

## 6 Limitations

Our findings are behavioral; nothing here measures subjective experience,
and "self" denotes only text generated by the same weights. The judge has
no episodic memory of writing; the test can only measure identification of
self-*typical* text. Scale conclusions are within one training recipe
(Qwen2.5) and one decoding setting (temperature 0.8 generation, greedy
judging). The paraphrase attack covers one representative cell (14B ×
Llama, three domains); the base-model ablation defines SELF as the
instruct sibling's text and uses a completion-style prompt, two deliberate
interpretive choices that limit direct comparison with chat-prompted
judges. Cohen's κ between judges and the perplexity rule is reported for
completeness but is uninformative under the observed marginal saturation
(§4). Human-foil
cells are smaller (41–147 pairs per domain) because length-matching
removes verbose-model-vs-concise-human pairs. Prompt datasets likely occur
in all models' pretraining data (shared across authors, but uncontrolled).
Extreme response biases make IPP sensitivity indices uninformative at
several sizes. All materials are English; per-cell power detects effects
≥ ~6 points above chance, and smaller effects may be missed.
