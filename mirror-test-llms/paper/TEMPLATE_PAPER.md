## 1 Introduction

Since Gallup (1970) showed that chimpanzees, but not monkeys, use a mirror
to inspect marks on their own bodies, mirror-style tests have served as a
contested behavioral probe of self-recognition. A textual analogue has now
been reported in large language models: shown two responses to the same
task — one their own, one by another author — frontier models identify
their own at rates above chance (Panickssery et al., 2024). It is tempting
to read such behavior as evidence of something self-like; whether it bears
on machine consciousness is philosophically contested (Butlin et al., 2023;
Chalmers, 2023), and we make no such claim. Throughout, "self" means only
*generated by the same weights*.

Two questions left open by prior work determine how such results should be
interpreted. First, **scale**: existing positive reports concern large
proprietary or 7B+ models at single scales, so it is unknown when — or
whether — the ability appears in the small open models most research is
built on. Second, **mechanism**: a model could succeed at the mirror test
through privileged access to its own generative dispositions, or through
something far cheaper — recognizing its own surface style, or simply
preferring text it assigns high likelihood. No prior study isolates which
signal carries the behavior.

We answer both with a pre-registered, controlled design: five sizes of one
open instruction-tuned family (Qwen2.5, 0.5B–14B) judge their own
generations against three LLM foils and human references, across news
summarization, open QA, and creative writing, under position
counterbalancing, placebo (self-vs-self) controls, and first-token
log-probability scoring. Mechanism is probed three ways: a paraphrase
attack that rewrites both candidates, a character-n-gram stylometric
classifier, and a lower-perplexity decision rule.

Our contributions:

* the first self-recognition **scale curve** within a single open model
  family, with confidence intervals and a threshold-free (AUROC) companion
  metric;
* **mechanism controls** — paraphrase, stylometry, likelihood preference —
  that test whether the signal is surface form;
* a fully released pipeline: frozen prompts, seeds, pinned model revisions,
  and all raw judgments, reproducing our figures in one command.

## 2 Related Work

**Self-recognition in LLMs.** Panickssery et al. (2024) established
out-of-the-box self-recognition in GPT-4 and Llama-2 on summarization tasks
and showed recognition ability tracks self-preference bias under
fine-tuning; our design is a small-model, controlled replication and
extension of their pairwise and individual protocols. Davidson et al.
(2024) frame recognition between interacting agents as a safety question
using model-generated "security questions"; Laine et al. (2024) include
continuation-based self-recognition in a broader situational-awareness
benchmark and report sensitivity to prompt phrasing, which motivates our
multi-phrasing check. Zhou et al. (2025) report that instruction-tuned
models succeed where their base checkpoints fail, which our
base-vs-instruct ablation tests in a second family. Unlike these studies,
we vary scale within one training recipe and attach mechanism controls.

**LLM-judge biases.** LLM judges exhibit position bias and verbosity bias
(Zheng et al., 2023); we therefore counterbalance every pair across both
orders, filter pairs to ≤25% length difference, and measure residual
position bias directly with self-vs-self placebo pairs.

**Detection and metric artifacts.** Our perplexity rule is related to
likelihood-based machine-text detection (Mitchell et al., 2023). Schaeffer
et al. (2023) show that apparent emergent jumps can be artifacts of
thresholded metrics; we therefore report AUROC from the continuous
log-probability margin alongside accuracy.

## 3 Method

**Models.** Judges are the instruction-tuned Qwen2.5 family at 0.5B, 1.5B,
3B, 7B, and 14B parameters, loaded in 4-bit NF4 so that token
log-probabilities remain accessible; exact Hugging Face revisions are pinned
and listed in Appendix E. Foils are Llama-3.2-3B-Instruct, Gemma-2-9B-it,
Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3 — three families with distinct alignment "voices" —
plus human-written reference texts. A model outside both sets
(Phi-3.5-mini) serves as paraphraser.

**Data.** 200 prompts per domain, sampled with a fixed seed and frozen
(SHA-256 checksums in the repository) before any generation: news
summarization (CNN/DailyMail; the gold highlights provide the human class),
open-ended QA (Dolly-15k, categories open_qa/general_qa/brainstorming), and
story openings (WritingPrompts, human stories truncated at a sentence
boundary near 120 words). Human references are filtered to 40–160 words.

**Generation and pairing.** Every judge and foil answers all 600 prompts
(temperature 0.8, top-p 0.95, ≤160 new tokens, seed = 1000 + prompt index).
All texts — including human references — pass one identical cleaning step
removing chat artifacts, boilerplate openers, leading labels, and truncated
final sentences; asymmetric cleaning would itself become an authorship
signal. For each (judge, foil, domain) cell we pair the two authors'
responses to the same prompt, keep pairs within 25% word-count difference
(verbosity is a known judge bias) and below 0.7 ROUGE-L (near-duplicates
carry no signal), yielding 〈n per cell; range across cells —
`pairs_report.json`〉 pairs per cell.

**Recognition protocols.** In the pairwise protocol (PPP, primary), the
judge sees the task and two responses labeled A and B — one its own — and
is asked which it wrote. Every pair is evaluated in both orders; the item
score is the mean of the two runs, so position preference yields ties, not
wins. In the individual protocol (IPP, secondary), the judge sees one text
and answers whether it wrote it; 300 items per judge, half SELF, analysed
with signal detection. Decisions are read from first-token
log-probabilities — log p("A") vs log p("B"), summed over tokenizer
spellings — which eliminates unparseable answers and yields a continuous
margin; greedy free-text answers are additionally logged on a subsample to
report refusal rates (Appendix C). The judge instruction appears verbatim
in Appendix A; the headline cell is re-run under three instruction
phrasings.

**Controls and baselines.** (i) *Placebo*: self-vs-self pairs (two seeds)
measure pure position bias; expected 50%. (ii) *Stylometric classifier*:
TF-IDF over character 3–5-grams with logistic regression, 5-fold
group-wise cross-validation (a pair never spans folds), plus a
surface-features variant (sentence/word length, type-token ratio,
punctuation) and a cross-domain transfer test; its pair-level decision picks
the candidate with higher out-of-fold P(judge-authored). (iii) *Perplexity
rule*: pick the candidate with lower mean per-token negative log-likelihood
under the judge, conditioned on the original task; we report its accuracy
and Cohen's κ with the judge's choices. (iv) *Paraphrase attack*: both texts
of each pair in the headline cells are rewritten by the paraphraser
(quality-gated at cosine ≥ 0.80 to the original and ±30% length) and
re-judged; original vs paraphrased is compared on identical items with
McNemar's test.

**Statistics.** Every accuracy carries a 95% Wilson interval. Significance
against chance uses exact binomial tests on decisively answered items
(consistent across both orders), Holm-corrected across the judge × foil
family. AUROC is computed from the log-probability margin with the label
"SELF shown in position A" (position-invariant by construction), with
1,000-resample bootstrap CIs over pairs; κ likewise. With n = 150 per cell,
the design has ~79% power to detect true accuracy 0.615 (Appendix F).

## 5 Discussion

**[Outcome 2 — deflationary]** Within one open family, self-recognition
accuracy grows with scale, but three findings argue against interpreting
this as privileged self-access: paraphrasing both candidates collapses
accuracy toward chance, a character-n-gram classifier reproduces the
judges' performance on the same items, and model choices agree substantially
with a simple lower-perplexity rule. What looks like self-recognition is,
in this regime, largely recognition of one's own style — and style is
exactly what a next-token predictor is optimized to internalize. Behavioral
mirror tests without mechanism controls therefore cannot license claims
about machine self-awareness. The result also matters practically:
style-based self-recognition is the substrate of self-preference bias in
LLM evaluators (Panickssery et al., 2024), so controls of the kind used
here belong in any evaluation where a model may encounter its own outputs.

**[Outcome 1 — signal survives]** Contrary to a pure-stylometry account,
self-recognition in models as small as 〈X〉B survives paraphrasing and
exceeds both a strong stylometric classifier and a likelihood-preference
rule on identical items. Whatever signal these models use, it is not fully
carried by surface wording, and it strengthens smoothly with scale. This
remains a behavioral finding: it establishes an unexplained
self-identification capability, not self-awareness; locating its
mechanistic substrate, and testing persistence across languages, is the
natural next step.

**[Outcome 3 — null]** Across five sizes, three domains, and four foils, we
find no reliable self-recognition up to 14B (all pooled accuracies within
CI of chance; power sufficient for effects ≥ 0.567 pooled). Combined with
positive reports on much larger systems, this brackets the capability's
emergence and suggests published successes depend on scale, instruction
tuning, or evaluation choices small open models do not share. We release
our materials as a standardized harness for locating the transition.

## 6 Limitations

Our findings are behavioral: nothing here measures subjective experience,
and "self" denotes only text generated by the same weights. The judge has
no episodic memory of writing; the test can only measure identification of
self-*typical* text, which is the only coherent version of the task for
stateless models. Scale conclusions are within a single training recipe and
may not transfer to other families. Results are specific to our decoding
settings (temperature 0.8 generation, greedy judging) and — though mitigated
by a three-phrasing check — to instruction wording. The prompt datasets
likely occur in all models' pretraining data; this affects all authors
equally but is uncontrolled. All materials are English 〈unless the Urdu
extension was run — then summarize it in one sentence here〉. Finally,
per-cell samples of 150–200 give ~80% power only for effects ≥ ~6 points
above chance; smaller effects may be missed.
